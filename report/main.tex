\begin{abstract}
    \noindent We study Krylov methods for the fast evaluation of graph filters of the form $ g(\L)s
    $, where $ \L $ is the (symmetric) graph Laplacian in the context of graph signal processing. We
    implement Lanczos and Arnoldi algorithm and we compare different orthogonalization strategies, together
    with a convergence-based stopping rule. We first discuss the computational costs of these
    variants. Then, through the experiments on Erdős-Rényi graphs we relate the iteration error
    to the true error. Finally, we report how runtime scales with graph size and sparsity.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

We introduce basic graph theory concepts and we recall some theoretical results used troughout the report.

\subsection{Signal processing on graphs}



We consider a weighted undirected graph $ \G = (\V, \E, \mathcal{W}) $,
where $ \V \subset \R^N $ is the set of vertices, $ \E \subset \R^M $ is the set of edges, and $ \mathcal{W}
: \V \times \mathcal{V} \to \R$ is a weight function. We represent the weight function with a $ N \times N $ matrix $ W $ such that 
$W_{i,j} = 
\begin{cases} 
    W(v_i, v_j), & \text{if } (v_i, v_j) \in \E \\ 
    0, & \text{otherwise} 
\end{cases} 
\quad \text{for all } i,j = 1, \dots, |\V|
$,
and for our needs, we assume $ W $ to be symmetric, that is $ W_{i,j} =  W_{j,i} $.


\begin{wrapfigure}{r}{6.5cm}
  \caption{Filtering signal on graphs.} 
  \label{fig:sigproc}
  \includegraphics[width=7.5cm]{assets/plots/printed_graphs.pdf}
\end{wrapfigure}

In the study of graph signal processing, we model the idea of sending a signal to a node as
assigning a value to a vertex with a function $ s : \V \to \R$, whose values can be represented by a vector
$ s = s(\V) = [s_1, \dots, s_N] \in \R^N $ where each entry $ s_i \defeq s(v_i)$ represents
the signal sent over a node $ v_i \in \V$. We also keep track of the weight of each vertex
with a function $ d(i) \defeq \sum_{j=1}^N W_{i,j} $, and we call $D =
\text{diag}(d(1), \dots, d(N))$ the degree matrix.

In this setting, we introduce the graph Laplacian $ \L = D - W$. By
construction, $\L^T = \mathcal{L}$, thus the Lanczos method can be safely applied to our 
problem. This matrix has some other useful properties that we will list in the following Proposition.
\begin{proposition}
  The Laplacian matrix $ \L \in \R^{N\times N} $ satisfies the following properties:
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item $ \L $ is symmetric and positive semi-definite.
    \item The smallest eigenvalue of $ \L $ is $ 0 $ with corresponding eigenvector the constant one
      vector $ \mathbf{1} $.
    \item $ \L $ has $ N $ non-negative real-valued eigenvalues $ 0 = \lambda_0 \le \lambda_1 \le
      \ldots \le \lambda_{N-1}$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
  See \cite{von2007tutorial}.
\end{proof}


\begin{remark}
  In light of this proposition and by the spectral theorem, the Laplacian can be decomposed as:
  \begin{equation*}
    \L = U \Lambda U^{*},
  \end{equation*}
  where $ U = \left[ u_0,\dots,u_{N-1} \right]$ is a $ N\times N $ orthogonal matrix and we call it
  the \emph{Fourier basis}, and $ \Lambda =
  \text{diag}\left(\lambda_0, \dots, \lambda_{N-1}\right)$ is the matrix of its eigenvalues, which
  are monotonically increasing, and the vectors in $ U $ are ordered according to the eigenvalues.
  \end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Graph filter]
  A graph filter is a continuous function $ g : \R^+ \to \R $. For our needs, we will assume $ g $ to be defined on the spectrum of $ \L $.
\end{definition}
Diagonalising the Laplacian would give an easy way to compute the function $ g(\L) $ by
evaluating it on the eigenvalues of $ \L $, which means computing $ g(\L) = U g(\Lambda) U^{*} $.
In matrix notation, applying a filter to a graph $ \G $ corresponds to the operation
\begin{equation*}
  s^\prime \defeq g(\L)s = U g(\L) U^{*}s.
\end{equation*}
However, computing the Fourier basis for $ \L $ is computationally expensive for large
graphs, as it requires the full eigendecomposition of $ \L $. This motivates the idea for using the Lanczos method for evaluating the matrix function $ g(\L)$.

We consider Erdős-Rényi and Sensor graphs, following PyGSP's \cite{pygsp} definitions.
\begin{definition}[Erdős-Rényi graph]
  \label{def:ER}
  We define the Erdős-Rényi graph $ \G(N,p) $ with $ N $ nodes, and probability $ p $, as the graph
  constructed by randomly connecting two nodes independently of any other edge, with probability $ p $.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Sensor graph]
  \label{def:Sensor}
  A sensor graph $ \G(N) $ is the graph constructed by picking $ N $ points on the square $ \left[
  0,1 \right] \times \left[ 0,1 \right] $ and connecting points with edges using $ k $ nearest
  neighbours.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
  As stated in \href{https://github.com/epfl-lts2/pygsp/blob/4716b12c470c579e3f477f2d1c1d99752c73252d/pygsp/graphs/nngraphs/sensor.py#L6}{PyGSP
    sensor graph source code}, the definition of Sensor graph changed in 2019, switching to
    generation via the k-means algorithm. This might give slightly different results from the 2015's article
    \cite{susnjara2015} we are trying to reproduce, but we don not investigate this further.
  
\end{remark}



\subsection{The Lanczos method}

\begin{definition}[Krylov subspace]
 Given a matrix $ A \in \R^{N \times N} $ and a vector $ b \in R^N $, the Krylov subspace of order
 $l$ is defined as the set $ \mathcal{K}_l (A,b) = \text{Span} \left(b, Ab, A^2b, \dots,
A^{l-1}b\right)  $. We represent the basis of this subspace with a matrix $ V_M = \left[ v_1,\dots,v_M
\right] \in \R^{N \times M}$.
\end{definition}
From the Arnoldi relation,
\begin{equation*}
  AV_l = V_lH_l + h_{l+1,l}v_{l+1}e_l^{*}, \\ \hspace{20pt}
  H_l = V_l^{*}AV_l =
  \begin{bmatrix}
    h_{11} & \dots & \dots & h_{1,l} \\
    h_{21} & h_{22} & & \vdots \\
           & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\
           & & h_{l,l-1} & h_{l,l}.
  \end{bmatrix},
\end{equation*}
we can derive the Lanczos method for a symmetric matrix $ A $. Setting
\begin{equation*}
\alpha_l \defeq h_{l,l},\hspace{20pt}\beta_l \defeq h_{l+1,l}.
\end{equation*}
From our hypothesis on $ A $, it follows that the matrix $ H_l $, which is both upper and lower Hessenberg 
matrix, is also symmetric; therefore it is tridiagonal---thus we rename it $ T_l $. Hence
the Arnoldi relation takes the form:

\begin{equation*}
  AV_l = V_l T_l  + \beta_l v_{l+1}e_l^{T}, \\ \hspace{20pt}
  T_l = V_l^{\top} A V_l = \begin{bmatrix}
    \alpha_1 & \beta_1 \\
    \beta_1 & \ddots & \ddots \\
      & \ddots & \ddots & \beta_{l-1} \\
      & & \beta_{l-1} & \alpha_l
  \end{bmatrix}.
\end{equation*}.

We now introduce a useful approximation for evaluating a matrix function using the Arnoldi projection.
\begin{proposition}
 Consider the filter $ g : [0, \lambda_{\text{max}}] \to \R$ and a signal vector $ s \in \R^N $.
 Then the Lanczos method approximates $ g(\L)s $ with: \footnote{This result holds in general for any function $f :\Omega \to \C$, where $ \Omega \subset \Lambda(A) $.}
\begin{equation}
 g(\L)s \approx  V_M \norm{s}_2 g(T_M) e_1 \eqdef g_M. \label{eq:projection}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}
Numerically, we often compute the eigendecomposition $ (\Lambda,U) $ from $ T_M $, and then
substitute $ g(T_M) = Ug(\Lambda)U^{\top} $ in the equation above.


\subsection{Algorithm}
\label{ssec:algo}

We begin by defining quantities that will be useful in describing our implementation of the algorithms.

\begin{definition}[Errors]
  \label{def:errors}
  We define the \emph{iteration error} as $\norm{g_{M+j} - g_M}_2 $, where $ j $ is small,
  and the \emph{true error} as $ \norm{e_M}_2 = \norm{g(\L)s - g_M}_2 $.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Error bounds]
  We indicate with $ \tau \eqdef \tau_{\text{STOP}} $ the algorithm stopping threshold for the
  inequality
  $ \| g_{M+3} -g_M \|_2< \tau $, and with $ \delta \eqdef \delta_{\text{ORTH}} $ the orthogonality
  threshold, i.e when $ \| V^{\top} V - I \|_2 < \delta $.
\end{definition}



In floating-point arithmetic, the basis $ V_M $ can loose orthogonality, and orthogonalizing
the incoming vector $ w $ only against $ v_j,v_{j-1} $ is insufficient, for example if the input
matrix $ A $ is badly conditioned. Hence, the need to
reorthogonalize the new vector $ w $ against all the previous basis vectors using some steps of the
Gram-Schmidt process. To do so, we implement three algorithms, which we refer to as Lanczos 
(Algorithm \ref{alg:lanczos}) and Arnoldi (Algorithm \ref{alg:arnoldi_double_ortho}), which we
implement following \cite{demmel1997} and \cite{susnjara2015}. These two algorithms are often called
by the orthogonalization selection in Algorithm \ref{alg:orthosel}, and include a stopping criterion
based on matrix function convergence. Before describing the algorithms, we explain how $ g_M $ is
evaluated in the Lanczos process using Equation \ref{eq:projection}.
Recall that $  g_M \defeq \|s\|_2V_Mg(T_M)e_1
$, where $ g(T_M) $ is computed by diagonalizing $ T_M $: because $ T_M $ is symmetric, real and tridiagonal, 
computing its eigendecomposition is stable and efficient; indeed the structure of $ T_M $ allows us
to use a divide and conquer algorithm for eigenvalues. We choose to represent it in banded form
(isolating $ \alpha_M $ and $ \beta_M $) and using \texttt{scipy.linalg.eigh\_tridiagonal} routine
from Scipy, which costs $ O(N^2) $.

\begin{algorithm}[ht!]
  \caption{Modified Lanczos method in floating point arithmetic.}
  \label{alg:lanczos}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Symmetric matrix $ A \in \R^{N\times N}$, vector $ b \in R^N $, $ M \in
    \N $, function $ g $, tolerance $ \tau $.
    \State \textbf{Output: } Basis matrix $ V_l = \left[ v_1,\dots,v_l \right] \in R^{N \times l} $ for $ K_l(A,b) $, vectors $
    \alpha =\left\{ \alpha_1,\dots,\alpha_l \right\} \in \R^l, \beta=\left[ \beta_1,\dots,\beta_{l-1} \right] \in \R^{l-1} $, where $ l \in
    \{ 1,\dots,M \}$ is the stopping iteration index.
    \State Initialise $ V \leftarrow \mathbf{0}^{N \times M} $ , $ \alpha \leftarrow \mathbf{0}^N $,
    and $ \beta \leftarrow \mathbf{0}^{N-1}$

    \State $ v_1 \leftarrow \frac{b}{\|b\|} $

    \For{$ j = 1, \dots, M-1 $}
    \State $ w \leftarrow Av_j  $
    \State $ \alpha_j \leftarrow v_j^{\top} w $

    \If{Partial reorthogonalization}
        \State $w \leftarrow w - \alpha_j v_j$
        \State \text{\textbf{if} } $j>0$ \text{ \textbf{then} } $w \leftarrow w - \beta_{j-1} v_{j-1}$\text{ \textbf{end
    if}}
    \ElsIf{Full reorthogonalization}
    \State $w = w - \sum_{i=1}^j (w^{\top}v_i)v_i $
    \State $w = w - \sum_{i=1}^j (w^{\top}v_i)v_i $
    \EndIf
    \State $ \beta_j = \|w\|_2 $
    \If{$ \beta_j = 0 $}
      \State 
      \Return $ V_j, \alpha_{1:j}, \beta_{1:j-1} $
      \Comment{--- Breakdown ---}
    \Else
      \If{$ \| g_j - g_{j-3}\|_2  < \tau$}
      \State 
      \Return $ V_j, \alpha, \beta $
      \Comment{--- Breakdown ---}
      \EndIf
      \State $ v_{j+1} = \frac{w}{\beta_j} $
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \Return $ V_M, \alpha_{1:M}, \beta_{1:M-1} $.
  \end{algorithmic}
  
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[ht!]
  \caption{Approximation of $ g(\L)s $ using Lanczos.}
  \label{alg:g_evaluation}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $ g $, Matrix $ T $, signal $ s $.
    \State $ \lambda, U \leftarrow $ diagonalize($ T $)  \Comment{Here $ \lambda $ is the vector of
    eigenvalues.}
    \State $ u_1 \leftarrow  (U^{\top})_{:,1}$
    \State $y \leftarrow \|s\| U (\text{Diag}(g(\lambda)) U^{\top})e_1 = \|s\| U (g(\lambda) \odot u_1)$
    \State \Comment{Here $ u \odot v $ denotes the element-wise product between
  two vectors $ u $ and $ v $.}
    \State \Return $ Vy $
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[ht!]
  \caption{Evaluation of $ g(\L)s $ using Fourier basis.}
  \label{alg:g_evaluation_fourier}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input:} Function $ g $, Graph $ \G $, signal $ s $.
    \State $ \Lambda,U \leftarrow \text{Fourier\_basis}( \G )$
    \State \Return $ U g(\Lambda) U^{\top}s $
  \end{algorithmic}
  
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[ht!]
  \caption{Orthogonalization selection.}
  \label{alg:orthosel}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \State \textbf{Input: $\delta = 10^{-10}$}.
    \State $V, T, j \leftarrow $ Lanczos(A,b, Partial)
    \If {$ \|V^{\top} V - I\|_2 > \delta $}
    \State $ V, T,j \leftarrow $ Lanczos(A,b, Full)
      \EndIf
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}




\subsubsection{Computational cost}
\label{ssec:cost}

Here we provide an overview of the computational cost of the algorithms we tested, for an input sparse matrix $ A $, where $ \text{nnz}(A) $ is the number of non-zero elements of $ A $:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Lanczos:} Computes $ V $  with partial orthogonalization, with $ O(M  N
  + M \cdot \text{nnz}(A)) $. Note that the product $ Av_j $ dominates the cost,
  and that $ \text{nnz}(A) = N^2 $ when $ A $ is dense. When we need to evaluate function $ g $ at
  each iteration, such as in Section \ref{ssec:ex23}, we must consider also the $ O(N^2) $
  operations to compute $ g(\L) $ with divide and conquer, thus the total cost in this case becomes $ O(M N^2) $.
\item \textbf{Lanczos with double orthogonalization:} It differs from Lanczos by the
  computation of $ w = w -
  \sum_{i=1}^j v_i(v_i^{\top}w) $, which can cost up
  to $ O(M N) $. Thus the total cost becomes $ O(M^2N +  M\cdot \text{nnz}(A)) $, and when we
  also evaluate $ g $ it becomes $ O(M^2N + MN^2) $.
\item \textbf{Arnoldi with double orthogonalization}: For the orthogonalization it takes $ O(M^2N)
  $, plus the matrix vector product at each step with $ A $, hence again $ O(M^2N + M \cdot
  \text{nnz(A)}) $. Cost is higher when evaluating $ g $, due to the fact that matrix $ H $ is not
  perfectly symmetric (it is up to a small epsilon) and not tridiagonal (it has some small
  perturbations outside the diagonals). Hence we must use the Schur-Parlett algorithm to compute the matrix function
  (which we use in the routine \texttt{scipy.linalg.funm}), which adds $ O(N^3)$ complexity per
  iteration, and in total $ O(MN^3) $ in the worst case.
 \end{itemize}

We observe that in Lanczos computing $ g $ it not necessarily a drawback when $ A $ is dense or when few iterations are performed.
It should also be noted that these algorithms suffer from a significant $ O(M N) $ memory cost for storing $ V_M $.

The associated Laplacian $ \L $ is terribly conditioned, indeed $ k_2(\L) = 2.365029616834158 \times 10^{17} $:

\subsubsection{Comparing Lanczos with Arnoldi}

Here we do a short numerical comparison of Lanczos and Arnoldi with double orthogonalization, which
should match the cost analysis from Section \ref{ssec:cost}. We do this to justify 
the need for using Lanczos with double orthogonalization rather that Arnoldi. In Algorithm
\ref{alg:arnoldi_double_ortho} we report our implementation of Arnoldi.

\begin{algorithm}[ht!]
  \caption{Arnoldi with double orthogonalization.}
  \label{alg:arnoldi_double_ortho}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
  \State \textbf{Input:} Matrix $ A \in R^{N\times N} $, $ s \in \R^N $, $ M \in \N$, function $
    g $, tolerance $ \tau $.
  \State \textbf{Output: } Basis matrix $ V_l = \left[ v_1,\dots,v_l \right] \in R^{N \times l} $
  for $ K_l(A,b) $, upper-Hessenberg matrix $ H \in R^{l \times l} $ where $ l \in
    \{ 1,\dots,M \}$ is the stopping iteration index.

    \State $ v_1 \leftarrow s / \|s\|_2 $
    \State Initialise $ V \leftarrow \mathbf{0}^{N \times M} $ and $ H \leftarrow
    \mathbf{0}^{M \times M} $
    \State $ V_{:, 1} \leftarrow v_1 $
    \For{$ j = 1 \dots M-1 $}
        \State $ w_1 \leftarrow A v_j $
        \State \Comment{First orthogonalization}
        \State $ h_1 \leftarrow V_j^\top w_1 $
        \State $ w_1 \leftarrow w_1 - V_j \cdot h_1 $
        \State \text{\textbf{if}} $w_1 = 0$ \text{ \textbf{then break} } \text{\textbf{end if}}
        \State $ v_{j+1} \leftarrow w_1 / \|w_1\|_2 $ 
        
        \State \Comment{Second orthogonalization pass}
        \State $ h_2 \leftarrow V_j^\top v_{j+1} $
        \State $ w_2 \leftarrow v_{j+1} - V_j \cdot h_2 $
        \State $ H_{1:j, j} \leftarrow h_1 + h_2 $
        \State $ H_{j+1, j} \leftarrow \|w_1\|_2 \|w_2\|_2 $\\
        
        \If{$ H_{j+1, j} = 0 $}
            \State \textbf{break} \Comment{Arnoldi breakdown}
        \EndIf
        
        \State \Comment{Convergence check}
        \If{$ j > 3 $ \textbf{and} $ j < M $}
            \If{$ \|g_j - g_{j-3}\|_2 < \tau $}
                \State \Return $ V_j, H_{1:j, 1:j} $
            \EndIf
        \EndIf
        
        \State $ v_{j+1} \leftarrow w_2 / \|w_2|_2 $
        \State $ V_{:, j+1} \leftarrow v_{j+1} $
    \EndFor
    
    \State \Return $ V_{:, 1:j}, H_{1:j, 1:j}$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

We consider the function $ g $ used in the experiments and $ N \in \left\{ 300, 400,
500, \dots, 6000\right\} $, $ M = 200 $ and $ p = 0.04 $. 
In Figure \ref{fig:avl} and Table \ref{table:avl}, we observe different correlations when running
the algorithm with residual break and until $ M $. In the former, described in Figure \ref{fig:avl_a}, Lanczos
with full orthogonalization outperforms the other two. In the latter in Figure

analysis due some eigenvalues of $ T_l $ being evaluated at zero by $ g $, as we will

evaluating the function.

\subsection{Comparing global error with iterate difference}
\label{ssec:ex1}

We want to numerically verify the approximation in Equation
\eqref{eq:projection} by relating the residuals in Definition \ref{def:errors}.
Let's recall some quantities that will be useful in this section: $ N $ is the number of graph
nodes and size of the Laplacian $ \L $, $ M $ is the maximum size of the Krylov subspace, and $ p $
the probability of the graph. 

\begin{experiment}
We set $ (N,M,p) = (500,200,0.04) $ and we try to replicate the Experiment 1 in Article \cite{susnjara2015}. 
\end{experiment}

Recall that computing $ g(\L)s $ is done by computing the Fourier basis for the graph $ \G $, which
is feasible for the small graph that we are considering. 



We consider the function $ g(t)
= \sin(\frac{1}{2}\pi \cos^2(\pi t)) \chi_{\left[ -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2} \right]}$, which we
adapt to the graph spectrum inteval $ [0, \lambda_{\text{max}}] $, and it is implmented in the \texttt{Itersine} class in Pygsp in Figure
\ref{fig:itersine}. Unfortunately, the authors in \cite{susnjara2015} apply an additional
transformation to $ g $ to match the eigenvalue distribution of $ \L $, which is showed in
\ref{fig:article_itersine}. We were not able to
reproduce it (despite many trials) as it seemed not documented both in the article and library.
Without this adaptation, when we evaluate the eigenvalues of $ T_l $ using $ g $, some eigenvalues
vanish, especially in the case of Erdős-Rényi graphs.
Using the algorithm
described in Section \ref{ssec:algo}, and computing the errors $ \|e_l\|_2 $ and $ \|g_{l+3} - g_l

Looking closely at Figure \ref{fig:ex1_estimate}: we immediately observe that the error curves are
close, and that their value is decreasing, which shows that the approximation of $ g $ gains
precision as the basis $ V_l $ gets larger. We also see how the eigenvalues of $ \L $ and $ T $ influence the steepness and smoothness of the curves: when eigenvalues fall outside $
[-\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}] $, we see that the curves rapidly reach machine precision: this is
unfortunately caused by issue explained above. We also
observe evident lack of smoothness in the iteration error curve: we believe that since $ g(t) = 0 $
for $ t\in \R \setminus \left[ -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2} \right]$, the eigenvalues of $ T $ tend
to jump in and out of the interval $ [-\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}] $, causing spikes. Hence, we found
that this method is sensitive to the eigenvalues distribution of the Laplacian. 

We also found interesting to visualize graphically as in Figure \ref{fig:sigproc} how the signal $ s $ is filtered by
the algorithm on the graphs used in this experiment, which corresponds to applying a low-pass filter.

\subsection{Performance comparison for Erdős-Rényi graphs}
\label{ssec:ex23}

In this experiment, we will study the performance correlation for running Lanczos on Erdős-Rényi
graphs $ \G(N,p) $ and comparing execution time when only $ N $ varies and when only $ p $ varies. 

\begin{remark}[Role of $ N $ and $ p $]
Erdős-Rényi graphs, the parameters $ N $ and $ p $ determine dimension and
sparsity of $ \L $, respectively. In fact, when $ N $ increases, we have a larger Laplacian and we expect higher computational cost due
to the increased cost of the matrix-vector multiplication $ \L v $ and to compute the eigenvalues of
$ T_M $. When $ p $ increases, $ \L
$ becomes more dense, requiring more floating point operations for the algorithm and so increasing
runtime. Figure \ref{fig:spy} shows how this property in a sample of the matrices.
\end{remark}

We choose $ \delta = 10^{-5} $, as we experimentally observed that smaller thresholds rarely
trigger full
orthogonalization. As in Algorithm \ref{alg:lanczos} our stopping criteria happens when $
\|g_l - g_{l-3}\|_2 < \tau $, at the step $
l$. \footnote{Here
index goes backwards because matrix $ T_j $ would not be defined for $ j>l $ at iteration $ l $!}

\begin{experiment}
In the following experiment, we generate two sample datasets, letting $ n = 6 $,:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $ N $ varies in $ \{ 250,500,\dots,2^n \cdot 250\} $, $ \tilde{p} = 0.04 $.
  \item $ p $ varies in $ \{ 0.01,0.02,\dots,2^n \cdot 0.01 \}$, $ \tilde{N} = 1000$.
\end{enumerate}
By abuse of notation we will often refer to the tuple $ (N,p) $ to indicate the columns of the datasets above as we are interested in correlating increasing couples of $ (N,p)  \in \{ (250,0.01), 
(500,0.02), \ldots,  (8000, 0.32)\}$.
We repeat the generation of these graphs $ 200 $ times to avoid bias from graph randomness.
 
\end{experiment}

When $ p $ varies the matrices become dense and we expect longer computational times, as the matrix
may lose orthogonality for dense matrices, but we see the opposite in Table

matrix $ \L $, which becomes dense as $ p $ grows, are mapped to zero by $ g $.

In general Figure \ref{sfig:loglog_g} shows a linear growth with few outliers, where times for
increasing $ N $ result slower than those of $ g $. Hence for this stopping criteria the size of $
\L $ influences execution time more than the sparsity. We believe that we could see a similar
pattern in case the algorithm runs with a different $ g $, as the added complexity is due the
evaluation procedure. In the next section we try to run
the procedure until the index $ M $, to verify how the error bound is influencing performance.

\section{Conclusions \& future work}

To conclude, we observed how bound and orthogonalization techniques can improve performance
trough early breaks, especially for large and dense matrices. However, without the precise
rescaling of $ g $, these bounds can start to be
unreliable and terminate the algorithm early, which may provide an insufficient approximation of the Krylov basis.
We didn't address how the high memory cost of $ O(N M) $ of Lanczos could be avoided by
using a two-step implementation, or how we could use selective reorthogonalization as described in
\cite{demmel1997} to reduce time complexity, because both are the scope of this work.
